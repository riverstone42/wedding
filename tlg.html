Carpool Lanes vs. Express Lanes

Since most of you will be getting around in a rental car, this guide is written with that in mind. Southern California freeways can be confusing because carpool (HOV) lanes and toll express lanes sometimes look very similar and may even share the same lane corridor — and tolls work a little differently when you're in a rental.

Please Read · Do Not Skip

It is very easy to drift onto a toll road by accident — please use caution.

Toll entrances can appear suddenly, and signage often gives you only a few seconds to react. If you are not paying close attention, you may end up on an Express Lane or toll road before you realize it.

If you do accidentally enter a toll road or Express Lane, you are still responsible for payment. An honest mistake does not waive the toll, and unpaid tolls can turn into added fees. In a rental car, the bill reaches you through the rental company rather than in your own mailbox.

How it works in a rental car — and what to do:

  • The bill comes through the rental company Because the license plate is registered to the rental company, the toll agency bills them first. They then charge the card on file for the toll plus their own administrative or "convenience" fee (often a per-day or per-toll charge that can exceed the toll itself). You don't typically receive the agency's own mailed notice.
  • Express Lanes may not be covered by your rental toll plan Even if you opted into the rental company's toll program, several LA-area Express Lanes (such as the 405, 91, 10, 110, and 15) are often excluded from those plans. An accidental trip on one of these can come back as a separate toll-by-plate charge or fee, so don't assume the plan protects you there. [1, 2]
  • What to do Ask at pickup exactly how tolls are handled and whether the Express Lanes are covered. Keep the card on file active for a few weeks after your trip, since toll charges often arrive after the car is returned. If you're unsure whether a toll was captured, contact the rental company rather than waiting.

Here is how the two lane types differ:

  • Carpool (HOV) lanes are generally free for vehicles meeting the posted occupancy requirements (typically 2 or more people, but requirements vary by freeway).
  • Express Lanes are toll lanes that charge a fee based on traffic conditions.
  • On some freeways, carpools may travel free or at a reduced toll in the Express Lanes, but the rules vary by location. Note that a rental car usually can't declare itself as a carpool the way a switchable transponder can, so it may be charged the full toll even with multiple people aboard.
  • If you enter an Express Lane in a rental car, the toll (plus any rental company fee) will be billed back to you through the rental company.

Important: Before using a lane marked "Express Lanes," check the signs carefully. If you are unfamiliar with Southern California tolling systems, the easiest option is to use your navigation app's "Avoid Tolls" setting.

Navigation Apps

Google Maps, Apple Maps, and Waze generally identify toll roads and Express Lanes. If you prefer not to deal with tolls, enable "Avoid Tolls" before starting your trip.

Wedding Guest Recommendation

Since you'll most likely be in a rental car for a few days, here's the simplest approach:

  1. At pickup, ask how the rental company handles tolls and whether the Express Lanes are covered. Opt into their toll program only if you expect to drive extensively and use toll roads on purpose.
  2. Otherwise, turn on "Avoid Tolls" in your navigation app and stick to the regular freeway lanes — this is the easiest way to sidestep surprise charges entirely.

Either approach avoids most toll-related issues while still allowing convenient travel throughout the Los Angeles and Orange County areas.
